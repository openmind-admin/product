I. The Challenge
We’re Building a Field, Not Competing in One
Product strategy in higher education dialogue is hard — not because the market is crowded, but because the market barely exists. There is no established category, no standard buyer, no proven playbook. CDI is simultaneously building the products, building the demand, and building the measurement infrastructure to prove any of it works.
No category to enter
Nobody is selling “campus culture change” as a product suite. Competitors own fragments — a workshop here, a survey there — but nobody holds the integrative frame.
No standard buyer
Our partners range from a provost with a vision to a student affairs VP with a budget to a state system with a mandate. The product must flex across all of them.
No proven ROI metric
We can’t point to an industry benchmark. We have to build measurement infrastructure that proves our own impact — and define what “success” even means for this work.
The closest thing to a "competitive landscape"
Braver Angels P3 FIRE P4 P5 Interfaith America P3 P5 Essential Partners P3 Heterodox Academy P1 P2 Culture Amp P5 model
Each touches one or two pillars. None operates across all five. None offers an institutional change framework. That’s both our moat and our burden — there’s no market to join, only one to build.
The implication: Our product strategy can’t follow conventional playbooks. We need a framework that organizes what we build, how we sell it, and how partners navigate it — all at once. That framework has two dimensions.

THE VISION: FACULTY CLASSROOM INTEGRATION (Q3 2026)
Build the connective tissue. The arc: awareness (Perspectives) → engagement strategies (Quiet Classroom) → handling difficult content (Contested Facts) → classroom norms and practice → async follow-up resources. The goal isn't another workshop — it's weaving constructive dialogue into the fabric of teaching so it becomes self-sustaining. A virtuous cycle where faculty practice begets student practice begets culture. Longer-term, an AI syllabus review tool could accelerate faculty adoption — but the sequenced pathway comes first.
From standalone workshops to an embedded professional development journey — this is how P2 becomes real.

Pillar 5 · Measurement and Organizational Learning
The Biggest Gap — and Biggest Opportunity
Funders have moved from requesting measurement to requiring it. CDI tells partners to measure their dialogue culture but provides no tools to do so. This is both our most urgent gap and our most exciting innovation space.
What exists: Perspectives Evidence Base (internal research on our own products). Program-specific surveys. But nothing partner-facing, and each program measured separately.
THE INNOVATION OPPORTUNITY
LLMs create an entirely new paradigm for measuring dialogue. Instead of static surveys and numeric scoring, the assessor can be a conversational AI — one that participates in and analyzes dialogue, adapts to context, generates narrative feedback, and can't be gamed the way traditional assessments can. What better way to measure dialogue than through conversation itself?

This connects to:
• Generative assessment — augments traditional measures with reflective narrative
• Measurement Learning Lab — partner-facing dashboard, benchmarking consortium
• Administrative data proxies — roommate change requests, speaker cancellations, cross-group participation — institutional data that already exists
This is where CDI can leapfrog the competition. Interfaith America, FIRE, and COFHE-style consortia are building traditional measurement. CDI has the opportunity to build something fundamentally different — measurement that embodies the dialogue it measures.
Timeline: Research landscape analysis (2026). Measurement Learning Lab (2027). Benchmarking consortium (2027-28).

Post-Program Sustainment IMPACT GAP

Pillars: ■ P3 Co-Curricular Experience + ■ P2 Curriculum and Pedagogy. Sustainment is what turns a program into lasting culture change. Without it, CDI delivers experiences that fade.

What's missing: Two related solutions: (1) Post-Perspectives practice — structured opportunities for graduates to practice dialogue skills across the academic year, facilitated issue-based discussions, DFC grad deployment as peer facilitators. These share infrastructure and should be designed together.

Modularity & Accessible Formats TIME + COST
Partners face two access barriers: programs are too expensive for smaller schools and too long for reluctant participants. Modularity solves both. — Liz, Naomi, Colleen/Tess, Kaleigh
Source: Converging signals from Partnerships, Live Programs, and CC. App State: "Students are not going to come to a 3-hour program — it needs to be an hour." Naomi: "Move implementation tools earlier in the pipeline." Liz: smaller committed partners can't afford Perspectives.

Pillars: ■ P2 Curriculum and Pedagogy + ■ P3 Co-Curricular Experience. Modular architecture serves both curricular (faculty embed) and co-curricular (advanced practice, shorter formats) contexts.

What exists: Perspectives as a monolithic 2.5-hr program. Live programs at 3–5 hours. Every sale requires institutional-level buy-in. No sub-one-hour option.

What's missing: A Perspectives overhaul — modular, personalized, adaptive. Shorter formats (sub-one-hour awareness products, async self-directed versions) solve the time barrier. Configurable live programs let partners skip modules they've covered. Per-seat pricing and a Perspectives Light version solve the cost barrier. Modularity is the single product architecture that addresses both access problems while enabling growth (institutions adopt pieces) and deepening impact (personalization + adaptivity).

Generative Assessment
Instead of "You scored 7/10 on perspective-taking," learners receive narrative reflections that surface patterns and generate questions. The assessment models what it measures.
Traditional: Numeric Scoring
Intellectual Humility: 6.2/10
Perspective-Taking: 7.8/10
Open-Mindedness: 5.4/10
Constructive Disagreement: 6.9/10

Overall: Above average. Consider practicing active listening.
Augmented: Generative Narrative
Across your Perspectives journey, a pattern emerged: you engage deeply and curiously when topics are abstract or unfamiliar — asking genuine questions, holding uncertainty well. But when topics connect to your personal experience, you tend to shift into advocacy earlier.

This isn't a flaw — it's a signal that proximity matters for you. The question worth sitting with: What would it look like to bring the same curiosity you show strangers' perspectives to the perspectives closest to your own?
The impact: Numeric scores flatten the very thing we're trying to develop. A "6.2 in intellectual humility" doesn't help anyone grow. A generative narrative that names a specific pattern and asks a question is the practice of dialogue — the assessment becomes an extension of the pedagogy. Augments traditional scoring across all CDI products starting 2027.

Train-the-Trainer Certification Pathway
How a campus professional goes from participant → certified CDI facilitator → institutional capacity that outlasts the partnership.
Certification Journey
1. Complete CDI Programs
Perspectives + Foundations + DFC — the existing product sequence. Prerequisite for certification.
2. Trainer Curriculum
CDI-specific training for trainers — facilitation theory, common failure modes, quality standards, adaptation guidelines. What CDI facilitators know beyond the content.
3. Supervised Facilitation
Two-tier model: in-person observation with a CDI master facilitator for initial sessions, then AI-augmented remote assessment for ongoing quality assurance. Scales supervision without proportionally scaling CDI headcount.
4. CDI Certification
Certified CDI Facilitator credential. LinkedIn badge. Annual license. Authorized to deliver Foundations and DFC independently on their campus.
5. Recertification & QA
Annual recertification. Measurement data on their sessions feeds back to CDI. Quality control without CDI in the room. Recurring licensing revenue.
